Root Causes

How water becomes contaminated

Four primary pathways introduce harmful pathogens and chemicals into drinking water supplies worldwide.

Unsplash License
01
Agricultural Runoff
Fertilizers, pesticides, and animal waste leach nitrates and pathogens into groundwater. Researchers have linked nitrate contamination to methemoglobinemia in infants and elevated cancer risk in adults.
Unsplash License
02
Industrial Discharge
Heavy metals, solvents, and toxic byproducts from manufacturing and mining enter waterways through inadequate disposal or accidental spills.
Unsplash License
03
Untreated Sewage
Where wastewater treatment is absent, raw sewage flows directly into rivers and aquifers, introducing E. coli, cholera, and typhoid pathogens.
Unsplash License
04
Aging Infrastructure
Corroding pipes leach lead and other contaminants into tap water. From 2014 to 2019, Flint, Michigan exposed more than 100,000 residents to elevated lead levels through failed municipal pipes.
Key Pathogens Transmitted Through Contaminated Water
Cholera
Vibrio cholerae; causes severe dehydration and can kill within hours if untreated
Typhoid
Salmonella typhi; kills 110,000–215,000 people globally per year
E. coli
Fecal coliform bacteria; a primary indicator of sewage contamination in water tests
Cryptosporidium
Chlorine-resistant parasite; causes over 748,000 cases in the U.S. annually

Sources: WHO Drinking Water Fact Sheet, 2023; CDC About Drinking Water, 2025

Pathways Forward

Three pillars of a global response

The WHO and UNICEF have identified proven strategies that, when implemented together, can significantly reduce water contamination's toll on human health.

Unsplash License
01
Water Treatment Systems
Point-of-use and community-scale filtration actively remove pathogens and chemical contaminants before water reaches households. The WHO has evaluated and certified these technologies since 2014.
Technology
Unsplash License
02
Quality Monitoring Networks
Real-time sensor networks detect contamination events before they become outbreaks. National programs that report to international bodies create accountability and enable rapid response.
Infrastructure
Unsplash License
03
Global Regulatory Frameworks
The WHO's Guidelines for Drinking-water Quality offer science-based standards countries can adopt into national law. UN Resolution 64/292 formally recognizes safe water as a universal human right.
Policy
Every $1 Invested in Safe Water & Sanitation Returns (WHO Estimate)
$5
in economic benefit through reduced healthcare costs, increased worker productivity, and fewer school days lost to preventable illness. Preventing outbreaks costs far less than treating them.
